Automata Intelligence's COO mandate is not simply to supervise a growing list of workflows. It is to ensure that every additional client, automation, and team member strengthens the company's ability to deliver reliable, adopted, and economically durable outcomes. Your role description's figures - more than 300 automations running monthly and more than 6,500 human hours saved - suggest that the company has already created meaningful velocity. The next operating challenge is to make that velocity repeatable without losing client intimacy, quality, or learning.

That is the kind of problem I have spent my career making visible and manageable. At Vape-Jet, I lead AI-first operating transformation across production, purchasing, quality, customer success, support, engineering issue flow, ERP/Odoo, CRM, collaboration systems, telemetry, standard work, and training in a software-enabled robotics environment. At Amazon, I led launch and execution for Prime Pittsburgh in a 24/7 customer-backward environment supporting approximately five million second-day orders annually, using mechanisms for labor, throughput, escalation, safety, quality, and customer experience. At Compunetics, I led engineering, manufacturing, quality, customer delivery, and high-reliability technical programs, building the controls and root-cause discipline required when failure has real consequences.

My AI and automation advisory work adds a complementary lens: opportunity is not enough. A scalable automation needs a defensible value promise, usable data and context, explicit human authority, evaluation, governance, adoption, and an owner who can sustain the workflow. As COO, I would translate those principles into a practical operating system connecting client promise, delivery health, run evidence, adoption, economics, workforce capacity, risk, and reusable patterns.

I would not pretend to know Automata's undisclosed revenue mix, pricing, gross-margin drivers, delivery model, support burden, or founder decision load from the outside. Those are first-order discovery questions. My proposed first 120 days begin by establishing that operating truth with leadership, teams, financial data, and representative clients before standardizing what should remain differentiated.

The thesis is straightforward: scale the rules, not the exceptions.

I would welcome the opportunity to test that thesis against Automata's actual priorities and learn what the company most needs from its next operating leader.
